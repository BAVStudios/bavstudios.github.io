Executive Summary

Enterprise Finance on a Part-Time Schedule

A part-time CFO is a senior chief financial officer who works with your business on a structured retainer, typically two to eight days per month, delivering strategic financial leadership without the $280K+ cost of a full-time hire. The engagement scales: lighter months might involve forecast updates and a director meeting, while heavier months cover capital raise preparation, board presentations, or acquisition modelling. This guide explains how the part-time model actually works in practice, what hours and cadence look like at different revenue stages, and how a part-time CFO compares to a full-time hire, a consultant, and your accountant. Whether you are searching for fractional CFO services or virtual CFO services, the underlying engagement structure is the same: senior finance leadership, scaled to fit.

The Problem

The All-or-Nothing Myth in Financial Leadership

Most business directors think about the CFO role in binary terms: either you hire a full-time chief financial officer or you do without one. That framing made sense when finance required a physical presence, paper ledgers, and daily oversight of a large accounting team. It makes no sense in 2026.

The reality is that most Australian SMEs between $2M and $20M in revenue need senior financial thinking for 15 to 40 hours a month. Not 160 hours. Not five days a week sitting in a corner office. They need someone who builds the forecast, models the next hire, prepares the board pack, and sits across the table from the bank. The rest of the month, the bookkeeper and finance manager handle operations.

✗ Full-time cost without full-time need: a $250K+ salary for someone who spends three days a week on work your bookkeeper could handle
✗ Talent drought: Robert Half's 2026 Salary Guide reports 94% of finance leaders experienced difficulty attracting and retaining staff to meet strategic goals
✗ Recruitment limbo: even if you budget for a permanent CFO, finding one willing to join a $5M-$15M SME takes three to six months and costs $30K-$60K in agency fees
✗ Doing without: the director fills the gap with gut instinct, spending hours each week on finance instead of customers and growth

The Engagement Model Shift

Global demand for fractional CFO services has doubled year-over-year, according to industry data. The question is not whether you need a CFO. It is whether you need one five days a week.

The Framework

The Engagement Ladder: How a Part-Time CFO Scales with Your Business

This is The Engagement Ladder. A part-time CFO engagement is not a fixed number of hours bolted onto your business. It is a structured model that scales across three stages, matching the intensity of financial leadership to where your business actually sits. Each rung delivers specific outcomes. You climb when the business demands it, not before.

1

Rung 1: Foundation (2-3 days/month)

Best for: $2M-$5M revenue

The CFO audits your chart of accounts, builds a management reporting pack, and creates a rolling 13-week cash flow forecast. Each month includes a close review, forecast update, and one 90-minute director strategy session.

Outcome: Clean data, reliable reporting within five business days of month-end.

2

Rung 2: Modelling (4-6 days/month)

Best for: $5M-$15M revenue

Everything from Rung 1, plus three-scenario budgets, profitability analysis, and regulatory impact models. The CFO begins attending board meetings and presenting to the bank alongside the director.

Outcome: Growth decisions backed by modelled data. The director presents scenarios, not guesses.

3

Rung 3: Commercial Partner (6-8 days/month)

Best for: $10M-$25M+ revenue or transaction-ready

The CFO leads board meetings, manages investor and bank relationships, and prepares the financial narrative for a capital raise or exit. This is the ceiling of the part-time model: beyond this, the business typically transitions to a full-time hire.

Outcome: Investor-grade financials. A CFO who sits across from acquirers, banks, or PE firms with a credible narrative.

What a Typical Month Looks Like: A $9M Services Business

Consider an engineering consultancy in Brisbane. Revenue: $9M. Staff: 28. The director has a bookkeeper in Xero, an external accountant for year-end compliance, and a finance coordinator handling invoicing and payroll. The business is growing at 18% annually and the director is evaluating a potential acquisition.

Weeks 1-2: Operational Rhythm

The part-time CFO reviews the month-end management accounts with the finance coordinator (2 hours). Produces a one-page commercial commentary highlighting margin trends across three service divisions. Updates the rolling 13-week cash flow forecast, flagging a tight week in six weeks when two large subcontractor payments coincide with a quarterly ATO obligation (3 hours). Records a 10-minute Loom video walking the director through the headline numbers.

Weeks 3-4: Strategic Work

The CFO builds a financial model for the acquisition target: a $3.2M environmental consultancy with 12 staff. Three scenarios modelled (base, synergy-realised, downside). Normalised EBITDA calculated. Working capital requirements mapped for the first 90 days post-acquisition. The CFO presents the model in a 90-minute director strategy session and prepares a preliminary bank presentation for a potential facility (8 hours across the fortnight).

Total CFO time that month: approximately 35 hours across five concentrated working days. The engagement fee: $7,500. A full-time CFO at SEEK's reported median of $225,000 base salary costs approximately $290,000 fully loaded (including the 12% superannuation guarantee, leave provisions, and payroll tax). That is $24,200 per month for a role that, in this business, requires strategic-level thinking for roughly one week out of four.

A part-time CFO is not a lesser version of a full-time CFO. It is the same seniority, the same capability, and the same strategic output, structured around the reality that most $2M-$20M businesses need CFO thinking for days per month, not days per week. The model matches the work to the need.

Is the Part-Time Model Right for Your Business?

Strong Fit for Part-Time

✓ Revenue between $2M and $20M with financial decisions growing in size and frequency
✓ You have operational finance covered (bookkeeper, BAS agent, external accountant) but nobody providing strategic oversight
✓ A capital raise, acquisition, or exit is within the next 24 months and you need the financial infrastructure to support it
✓ You want a CFO embedded in your decision cycle, not a consultant who delivers a report and disappears
✓ You already use cloud accounting (Xero, MYOB, or QuickBooks) and your team communicates via video and messaging platforms

Consider Full-Time Instead If:

✗ Revenue exceeds $20M with daily finance complexity requiring a CFO on-site five days per week
✗ You need someone to manage a large finance team (five or more staff) with hands-on daily supervision
✗ Your business is listed or preparing for IPO, where continuous disclosure obligations require a permanent C-suite appointment
✗ Revenue is under $1M with simple finances. A good bookkeeper and external accountant will serve you well at this stage

The part-time model works because it matches how CFO-level work actually occurs in a business between $2M and $20M. Strategic finance is not a steady daily activity. It clusters around monthly closes, quarterly forecasts, annual budgets, and event-driven projects. Fractional CFO services are structured around those natural rhythms. A fractional CFO delivers the same output as a permanent hire, just without the permanent cost.
